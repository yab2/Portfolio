Network Connection Troubleshooting Guide

Step-by-step solutions to fix internet connectivity issues

Is your internet not working? Don't worry! This guide will walk you through 8 simple troubleshooting steps to get your network connection back online.

1

Check If Other Devices Can Connect

First, find out if the problem is with one device or your entire network.

  • Try connecting another device (phone, tablet, or laptop) to your Wi-Fi
  • If other devices work fine, the problem is with your original device
  • If nothing connects, the issue is with your router or internet service
Pro Tip:

Test both Wi-Fi and mobile data on your phone to confirm if the internet itself is working.

2

Reboot Your Router and Modem

Restarting your network equipment fixes most connection problems.

  • Unplug the power cable from your router and modem
  • Wait for 30 seconds
  • Plug the modem back in first and wait for it to fully start
  • Then plug in the router and wait 1-2 minutes
  • Try connecting to the internet again
Why This Works:

Rebooting clears temporary errors and refreshes the connection between your devices and the internet.

3

Check Your Wi-Fi Settings

Make sure you're connected to the right network with the correct password.

  • Open your Wi-Fi settings on your device
  • Check if you're connected to your home network
  • If you see "Connected, no internet", disconnect and reconnect
  • Make sure you entered the correct Wi-Fi password
4

Check Network Adapter Properties

On Windows, verify your network adapter is working correctly.

  • Right-click the network icon in your taskbar
  • Select "Open Network & Internet settings"
  • Click "Change adapter options"
  • Right-click your Wi-Fi or Ethernet connection
  • Select "Properties" to check settings
What To Look For:

Make sure "Internet Protocol Version 4 (TCP/IPv4)" is checked.

5

Reset Your IP Settings

Setting your device to get an IP address automatically often fixes connection issues.

  • Open Network Properties (see Step 4)
  • Double-click "Internet Protocol Version 4 (TCP/IPv4)"
  • Select "Obtain an IP address automatically"
  • Select "Obtain DNS server address automatically"
  • Click OK and restart your computer
6

Run Windows Network Troubleshooter

Windows has a built-in tool that automatically finds and fixes common problems.

  • Click the Start menu and type "troubleshoot"
  • Select "Troubleshoot settings"
  • Click "Network and Internet"
  • Click "Run the troubleshooter"
  • Follow the on-screen instructions
Quick Tip:

The troubleshooter will tell you what it found and if it fixed the problem.

7

Update Network Drivers

Outdated drivers can cause connection problems. Keep them up to date.

  • Right-click the Start menu and select "Device Manager"
  • Expand "Network adapters"
  • Right-click your Wi-Fi adapter
  • Select "Update driver"
  • Choose "Search automatically for drivers"
8

Install Windows Updates

Windows updates often include network fixes and improvements.

  • Click the Start menu and select "Settings"
  • Click "Update & Security"
  • Click "Windows Update"
  • Click "Check for updates"
  • Install any available updates and restart your computer
Important:

Always restart your computer after installing updates to apply all changes.

Still Having Issues? If none of these steps work, contact your Internet Service Provider (ISP). The problem might be on their end, or you may need professional help.
